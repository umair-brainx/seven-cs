Seven Cs of Communication

For Tech Services Teams

Course Overview and Objectives

Effective communication is crucial in software development. It can make or break your project

Why This Matters: Miscommunication can derail projects. Poor communication often leads to misunderstandings, delays, and even project failure in tech teams.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply the Seven C's to day-to-day communication (email, Slack, Jira, PRs, docs)
  • Spot and fix unclear, incomplete, or needlessly long messages
  • Write bug reports, status updates, and client emails that reduce back-and-forth
  • Give and receive feedback with professionalism and empathy

Seven C's Overview

The Seven C's framework is a checklist for effective communication, adapted specifically for tech industry contexts.

When in doubt, especially with complex or technical topics, it's better to err on the side of being complete even if it means adding a bit more detail — completeness should not be sacrificed for brevity if it risks misunderstanding.

Clear
Easy to understand, free of ambiguity — use simple language and focus on key points
Concise
Brief and to the point — remove unnecessary details
Concrete
Specific and tangible — supported by facts or examples
Correct
Accurate and error-free — both in content and form (no factual or grammatical mistakes)
Coherent
Logical and well-structured — information flows in an organized way
Complete
Contains all necessary information — so the audience has everything needed to understand the message
Courteous
Polite and positive — respectful tone, mindful of the audience's perspective

1. Clarity — Clear Communication

Definition: Your message is immediately and easily understood, with no ambiguity. You convey exactly what you mean using common words (or defined technical terms), clear sentence structure, and proper context.

Bad Example

"The module isn't working right; fix it ASAP."

Issues: Which module? What is "not working"? Lacks specifics and context.

I was given brief and vague information, resulting in a lot of time spent trying to understand someone else's riddle

— Developer feedback
Good Example

"The payment processing module throws a NullReferenceException when a user submits the checkout form (see attached log excerpt). Expected: transaction completes with a confirmation message. Actual: users see an error page. Please investigate this bug today as it's blocking all credit card payments."

Practical Tips for Clarity

  • Know your purpose before you communicate
  • Use plain language, avoid jargon when possible
  • Be specific with names, dates, and figures
  • Confirm understanding with your audience

2. Conciseness — Concise Communication

Definition: Brief and to the point without losing essential information. Brevity should not come at the expense of meaning.

Too Verbose

Long, rambling message about login errors that could be much shorter...

Just Right

Issue: Users can't log in since yesterday's deployment.
Action: Rolling back to previous version now. ETA: 30 minutes.

Practical Tips for Conciseness

  • Stick to the point — one message, one topic
  • Avoid redundancy and filler words
  • Use bullet points for multiple items
  • Edit ruthlessly before sending

3. Concreteness — Concrete Communication

Definition: Specific, tangible, and supported by evidence. Use numbers, examples, and facts instead of vague generalities.

Too Abstract

"Our performance significantly improved."

Concrete

"Improved page load time from 4s to 2s — a 50% increase in performance. Database query optimization reduced response time by 1.2 seconds on average."

Practical Tips for Concreteness

  • Use specific numbers, dates, and metrics
  • Avoid vague terms like "soon," "better," "many"
  • Provide concrete examples to illustrate points
  • Include error codes, version numbers, and system details

4. Coherence — Coherent Communication

Definition: Logical and well-structured information flow. Ideas connect naturally and follow a clear sequence.

Practical Tips for Coherence

  • Organize information in logical sequence
  • Use clear transitions between topics
  • Group related information together
  • Structure emails and documents with clear sections

5. Correctness — Correct Communication

Definition: Accurate and error-free in both content and form. This builds trust and credibility with your audience.

Practical Tips for Correctness

  • Proofread for grammar and spelling errors
  • Fact-check technical details and version numbers
  • Verify names, dates, and figures
  • Have colleagues review important communications

6. Completeness — Complete Communication

Definition: Contains all necessary information so the audience has everything needed to understand and act on the message.

Use the 5W+H framework: Who, What, Where, When, Why, How

Practical Tips for Completeness

  • Include all context needed for understanding
  • Provide reproduction steps for bug reports
  • Include timelines and deadlines in project updates
  • Anticipate and answer likely follow-up questions

7. Courtesy — Courteous Communication

Definition: Respectful, considerate, and positive in tone. Shows empathy and cultural sensitivity.

Develop empathy... respect and understand others to make sure they feel comfortable

Elements of Courteous Communication

  • Positive tone: Avoid harsh or accusatory language
  • Respect and empathy: Consider the recipient's perspective
  • Basic politeness: Use "please" and "thank you"
  • Avoid blame: Focus on solutions, not fault-finding
  • Cultural sensitivity: Be mindful of different backgrounds and time zones

Practical Takeaways

Clear Be easily understood
Concise Be brief and impactful
Concrete Be specific and tangible
Correct Be accurate and error-free
Coherent Be logical and organized
Complete Be thorough
Courteous Be respectful and friendly
