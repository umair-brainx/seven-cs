Course Overview and Objectives

Effective communication is crucial in software development. It can make or break your project

  • Apply the Seven C's to day-to-day communication (email, Slack, Jira, PRs, docs)

Communication Crisis Case Study: The Mars Climate Orbiter

$125 Million Failure

Background: NASA's $125 million Mars Climate Orbiter crashed in 1999 due to a communication error between teams using different measurement systems (imperial vs. metric).

Communication Breakdown Analysis

❌ Lack of Clarity

Measurement units weren't explicitly communicated between teams

❌ Incomplete Information

Documentation didn't specify unit standards or conversion requirements

❌ Poor Verification

No cross-team validation process to catch the discrepancy

❌ Missing Completeness

Critical technical specifications were assumed, not documented

🚀 Lessons for Tech Teams

  • Always specify units, formats, and standards in technical communications
  • Implement cross-team review processes for critical integrations
  • Create shared glossaries for technical terms and specifications
  • Establish verification checkpoints for critical information exchange

Seven C's Overview

The Seven C's framework is a checklist for effective communication, adapted specifically for tech industry contexts.

When in doubt, especially with complex or technical topics, it's better to err on the side of being complete even if it means adding a bit more detail — completeness should not be sacrificed for brevity if it risks misunderstanding.

These principles are universal but we will apply them to scenarios relevant to Tech teams. For example, a complete communication for a developer might mean including code version and steps to reproduce in a bug report (not just "it doesn't work"), whereas for a project manager it might mean providing timelines, not just saying "project delayed."

Clear
Easy to understand, free of ambiguity — use simple language and focus on key points
Concise
Brief and to the point — remove unnecessary details
Concrete
Specific and tangible — supported by facts or examples
Correct
Accurate and error-free — both in content and form (no factual or grammatical mistakes)
Coherent
Logical and well-structured — information flows in an organized way
Complete
Contains all necessary information — so the audience has everything needed to understand the message
Courteous
Polite and positive — respectful tone, mindful of the audience's perspective

1. Clarity — Clear Communication

Definition: Clarity means your message is immediately and easily understood, with no ambiguity. You convey exactly what you mean. In practice, this means using common words (or defined technical terms), structuring sentences clearly, and ensuring context is provided.

Bad Example

"The module isn't working right; fix it ASAP."

Issues: Which module? What is "not working"? Lacks specifics and context.

Good Example

"The payment processing module throws a NullReferenceException when a user submits the checkout form (see attached log excerpt).

Expected: transaction completes with a confirmation message.

Actual: users see an error page.

Please investigate this bug today as it's blocking all credit card payments."

I was given brief and vague information, resulting in a lot of time spent trying to understand someone else's riddle

— Developer feedback

Simple requests like 'set up the environment' could not be enough when you are texting someone you have known only for a few weeks or an inexperienced intern

— Developer insight

In tech teams, what one person thinks is a clear message can often be interpreted differently by another. The clarity leaves little room for misinterpretation.

Know your purpose

Before communicating, identify what you need from the audience and make that explicit (e.g., "Action Required: ...")

Use plain language

Avoid unnecessary jargon or explain it if the audience isn't familiar

Be specific

Names, dates, figures, and references add clarity

Confirm understanding

Ask for summarization or encourage questions

2. Conciseness — Concise Communication

Definition: Concise communication is about keeping your message brief and to the point. It means removing filler words, redundancies, and sticking to what's essential. In our tech context, this helps busy colleagues and clients grasp your point quickly without wading through unnecessary detail.

The Balance with Clarity: Emphasize that concise does not mean cutting important information. It's about efficiency of words, not omitting needed content. "Brevity should not come at the expense of meaning" – we still need completeness (more on that later) even as we strive for brevity. A clear, complete message that's a bit longer is better than a short but confusing one. However, often we can have both clarity and brevity by smart editing.

Many times, I was sure I gave a detailed description of the problem, [but] my message was too complicated... and often too long!

— Developer reflection
Too Verbose

"Hi team, I just wanted to let you know that I have noticed an issue that seems to be causing problems with our software application. It appears that when a user tries to log in to the system using their credentials, occasionally, not always but in some cases, an error message will pop up and it might say something along the lines of 'unexpected system exception occurred, please contact admin'. I think this might be something we should look into when possible, if everyone agrees."

Just Right

Issue: Intermittent login errors for users. An 'Unexpected system exception' message appears for some users after entering credentials.

Action: Needs investigation – please confirm if you can replicate this bug.

Practice Activity: Conciseness

Task: Condense this message:

"At this point in time we are in a situation where the database is experiencing an overload of requests, which is having the effect of causing our website to respond in a very sluggish manner."

Question: How would you rephrase this concisely?

Better version: "Database overload is causing slow website response times."

Stick to the point

Identify the core message and make it prominent. One message = one topic

Avoid redundancy

Don't repeat the same idea in different words. Remove filler words ("just," "actually," "basically")

Use bullet points

Forces brevity and improves scannability for multiple items

Edit ruthlessly

Review and cut unnecessary words before sending

3. Concreteness — Concrete Communication

Definition: Concrete communication means your message is specific, tangible, and supported by evidence. It's the opposite of being vague. When you communicate concretely, the audience can visualize or understand exactly what you mean, because you've given details or examples to anchor your message. In technical work, this often means using data, examples, or clear descriptions rather than abstract statements.

Why it matters: Concrete information improves understanding and retention. A tester's bug description with concrete details (error codes, screen images, steps) will get a faster fix than one that says "feature X is broken." Similarly, a project status update like "We completed 8 out of 10 tasks this sprint" is more actionable than "We made some progress." Concrete = clarity + credibility.

Too Abstract

Abstract: "The new release significantly improved performance."

Abstract: "The app fails under certain conditions."

Concrete

Concrete: "The new release improved page load time from 4s to 2s on average – a 50% performance increase."

Concrete: "When more than 200 users log in simultaneously, the app returns 'Service Unavailable (503)' errors about 30% of the time."

Interactive Activity: Concreteness

Task: Help "concretize" this vague statement: "Our client is unhappy with the project."

Question: What specific facts would make this statement more concrete?

Better versions:

  • "Client X reported that the last deliverable missed 3 key requirements and was delivered 2 weeks late, leading to their dissatisfaction"
  • "Client survey feedback score dropped from 9 to 6 this month, indicating unhappiness with our project's progress."

Use numbers or data

Quantify statements (e.g., "5 out of 7 tests failed")

Provide examples

Give real examples rather than abstractions

Avoid vague qualifiers

Replace "soon," "a lot," "better" with specifics

Visual aids

Use screenshots, diagrams, or code snippets in technical communication

Exercise: Good vs. Bad Communication

Applying Clear, Concise, Concrete principles

Major Exercise - Message Analysis

Bad Message

"Hey, I need that thing ASAP. The last build was terrible. There are a lot of problems in the system. Can you fix everything and update me? This is urgent!!"

Analysis: This message doesn't specify what "thing" or "problems," it's rude/pressuring ("terrible... fix everything"), and gives no concrete detail or clear action.

Good Message (Rewrite)

"Hi [Dev Team], I tested Build v1.2.3 and found several issues:

  1. The login page crashes on incorrect password (no error message shown).
  2. Order summary is not updating when items are removed from the cart.

These are high-priority bugs because they impact the release timeline. Could we prioritize fixing these within the next 2 days? Please let me know if you need more details or help reproducing them. Thank you!

Analysis: This rewrite is clear (specific features and issues), concise (bullet-pointed issues, no rambling), concrete (specific pages and actions), complete (lists what needs fixing, why it's important, and what is requested), and courteous in tone.

4. Coherence — Coherent Communication

Definition: Coherent communication means that all parts of your message are logically connected and consistent. The message has a flow – it's well organized, and each point follows sensibly from the previous. Coherence also means your tone and information are consistent (no contradictions). Essentially, the audience should be able to follow your train of thought from start to finish.

Importance in Tech: In software teams, coherence is crucial when explaining complex ideas (like architecture decisions, or test plans). If you jump disjointedly between points, or present information out of order, people get lost. A coherent structure helps – e.g., when writing a design proposal, you might follow a structure: Background → Problem → Proposed Solution → Benefits → Next Steps. If these elements are jumbled, the reader will be confused.

Incoherent

"...We might miss the deadline. The performance test results are attached. By the way, I think the login bug is resolved now. Earlier, we discussed adding more logging. Let's focus on the deadline – any ideas? The server load was high last week."

Dividing your message into sections and subsections can work miracles... Text editing tools are very useful for making the message more digestible

— Developer tip

Logical structure

Follow clear structure (chronological, cause-and-effect, problem-solution)

Use connectors

Words like "first, secondly, finally," "however," "therefore"

One theme at a time

Don't mix unrelated topics

Consistent terminology

Use same terms for same concepts throughout

5. Correctness — Correct Communication

Definition: Correct communication means that the information you convey is accurate, error-free, and appropriate. This covers factual correctness (the content is true and up-to-date) and technical correctness (grammar, spelling, punctuation, and format). If a message isn't correct, it can mislead or distract the audience. As Indeed's article notes, "incorrect information can reduce your credibility" and errors will distract or erode trust. In tech, where details matter (version numbers, API endpoints, specifications), correctness is vital.

Why it matters: Consider an email to a client with a small typo in a version number or an incorrect date — it can cause a big misunderstanding (e.g., they prepare for the wrong release). Or a requirements document with a mis-stated constraint can lead developers down the wrong path. Internally, if your Slack message has confusing grammar or missing punctuation, colleagues might misinterpret instructions.

Confusing

"Install Visual Studio Code npm Angular Git Docker We will configure later"

Classic example: "Lets eat Grandma"

Clear

"Install Visual Studio Code, npm, Angular, and Git/Docker. We will configure later."

Fixed version: "Let's eat, Grandma"

Meaning Inversion Examples: Small errors can completely change meaning:

  • "users unable to log in" vs "users are now able to log in"
  • Missing punctuation can cause confusion in technical instructions

Areas of Correctness

  • Grammar and Spelling: Basic correctness matters for professionalism
  • Factual Accuracy: Double-check numbers, dates, and facts
  • Correct Format/Audience: Match communication style to medium and audience

Proofread

Check for grammar and spelling errors before sending

Fact-check

Verify technical details, version numbers, and data

Verify details

Double-check names, dates, and figures

Colleague review

Have others review important communications

6. Completeness — Complete Communication

Definition: Complete communication means your audience has all the information they need from your message, without needing to ask follow-up questions. It answers the basic questions (who, what, where, when, why, how) relevant to the topic. A complete message provides context, key details, and next steps so nothing essential is missing. If clarity is about being understood, completeness is about being thorough. This is arguably the most crucial 'C' in technical teamwork because missing information can cause errors, rework, or delays. We intentionally emphasize Complete because tech teams often suffer when details are left out.

The Cost of Incomplete Info: A QA engineer files a bug report but forgets to include the test environment or steps to reproduce. What happens? The developer cannot replicate the bug, and a back-and-forth ensues: dev asks for more info, QA adds details, maybe days pass – productivity drops. Research from a developer forum found that "the team responsible for fixing a bug often receives a report that isn't very detailed... incomplete reports lack steps to reproduce or technical specs, leading to an eternal ping-pong of messages to request missing details". This "ping-pong" wastes time and frustrates everyone. By contrast, a complete bug report (with all fields filled: steps, expected vs actual result, screenshots, environment, etc.) can be picked up and resolved much faster with minimal clarification needed.

5W+H Framework

Use this checklist to ensure completeness:

  • Who: Who is involved? Who should act?
  • What: What exactly needs to be done?
  • Where: Where does this apply (which system, environment)?
  • When: When is the deadline? When did this occur?
  • Why: Why is this important? Why now?
  • How: How should it be done? How to reproduce?

Group Activity: Completeness

Scenario: Practice identifying missing information in typical tech communication scenarios

Examples of complete communication:

  • For developers: Include code version and steps to reproduce in bug reports (not just "it doesn't work")
  • For project managers: Provide timelines, not just "project delayed"
  • Bug report scenario: Tester's description with concrete details (error codes, screen images, steps) gets faster fixes than "feature X is broken"

Include all context

Provide all context needed for understanding

Reproduction steps

Include step-by-step instructions for bug reports

Timelines & deadlines

Always include when information in project updates

Anticipate questions

Think of likely follow-up questions and answer them preemptively

7. Courtesy — Courteous Communication

Definition: Courteous communication means communicating with respect, positivity, and empathy. It's about the tone and attitude of your message – being friendly and considerate of the recipient's feelings and perspectives. Even when delivering criticism or bad news, courteousness ensures professionalism. This also includes being mindful of cultural norms when working with international colleagues or clients (e.g., forms of address, level of directness, etc.). Essentially, treat the reader/listener as you would like to be treated, with politeness and consideration.

Why Courtesy Matters (Especially in Tech Teams): In high-pressure environments (tight deadlines, production outages), tempers can flare and communication can turn curt or hostile – but that usually backfires. A disrespectful code review comment or a rude email can demoralize the team and damage relationships. Conversely, courteous communication fosters a positive team culture and better collaboration. Externally, courtesy is crucial for client satisfaction – how we communicate is a direct reflection of our company's professionalism. Even if we have bad news (like a delay), delivering it courteously (apologizing for inconvenience, showing understanding of the client's position) can maintain trust.

Develop empathy... respect and understand others to make sure they feel comfortable

Elements of Courteous Communication

  • Positive tone: Avoid harsh or accusatory language
  • Respect and empathy: Consider the recipient's perspective
  • Basic politeness: Use "please" and "thank you"
  • Avoid blame: Focus on solutions, not fault-finding
  • Cultural sensitivity: Be mindful of different backgrounds and time zones

Personal Communication Improvement Plan

Now that you've learned the Seven Cs, it's time to create your personalized roadmap for improvement. Each participant should develop a customized plan focusing on practical application in their daily work.

📝 Your Personal Communication Action Plan

Use this framework to create your individualized improvement strategy:

1. Self-Assessment Know Your Starting Point

Identify personal communication strengths and weaknesses

  • Which of the 7 Cs do you naturally excel at?
  • Which ones challenge you most?
  • What feedback have you received about your communication?
  • Review recent emails, Slack messages, or documents you've written
2. Goal Setting Focus Your Efforts

Choose 2-3 specific areas for improvement

  • Select your top 2-3 weakest Cs to work on
  • Make goals specific and measurable
  • Example: "Write more complete bug reports with all 5W+H elements"
  • Example: "Reduce email length by 30% while maintaining clarity"
3. Practice Opportunities Apply Daily

Identify daily situations to apply the 7 Cs

  • Morning standup updates
  • Code review comments
  • Client email responses
  • Bug reports and incident updates
  • Slack communications with team
4. Feedback Mechanisms Get Input from Others

Establish ways to get feedback on communication effectiveness

  • Ask colleagues to review important emails before sending
  • Request feedback on meeting presentations
  • Create a buddy system for communication accountability
  • Survey clients/stakeholders on communication clarity
5. Progress Tracking Measure Your Growth

Set measurable milestones and review dates

  • Weekly self-check: "How did I apply the 7 Cs today?"
  • Monthly review of saved communications
  • Quarterly feedback collection from team members
  • Track metrics: email response rates, meeting effectiveness

Remember: Communication improvement is a journey, not a destination. The Seven Cs provide your roadmap, but consistent practice and reflection will get you there.

Practical Takeaways Summary

Clear Be easily understood

Define your purpose and stick to it. Use simple language so even new team members grasp your message. Before sending, ask "Could someone misunderstand this?"

Concise Be brief and impactful

Cut unnecessary words and get to the point quickly. Use bullet points for readability. Respect everyone's time while conveying needed info.

Concrete Be specific and tangible

Provide examples, data, or details to support points. Replace vague terms ("soon," "a lot") with specifics ("by Monday 5 PM," "increase of 25%").

Correct Be accurate and error-free

Double-check facts, figures, and grammar. Proofread important communications to avoid embarrassing mistakes that erode trust.

Coherent Be logical and organized

Present information in sensible order with natural flow. Group related topics together. Use headings or transitions to guide the reader.

Complete Be thorough

Include all relevant information so receivers aren't left with questions. Anticipate what your audience needs to know. Before sending, check: "Did I include everything important?"

Courteous Be respectful and friendly

Show respect for the reader's time and perspective. Use positive language and be mindful of tone, especially in written communication where tone can be misinterpreted.
